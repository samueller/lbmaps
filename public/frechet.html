The above visualization plots the Fréchet inequalities1. I hadn't seen these inequalities visualized before. Hopefully, this is of help to people studying and using probability bounds. These bounds have wide application, including logic2, artificial intelligence3, statistics4, quantum mechanics5, and reliability theory6. The plots display lower bounds, upper bounds, range, and exact values of P(A, B) (conjunction) or P(A ∨ B) (disjunction) on the unit square with axes P(A) and P(B). The following inequalities are used:
Truth FunctionInequality
Conjunctionmax(0, P(A) + P(B) − 1) ≤ P(A, B) ≤ min(P(A), P(B))
Disjunctionmax(P(A), P(B)) ≤ P(A ∨ B) ≤ min(1, P(A) + P(B))

These are special cases (n = 2) of Fréchet inequalities: 

max{0, P(A1) + P(A2) + … + P(An) − (n − 1)} ≤ P(A1, A2, …, An) ≤ min{P(A1), P(A2), …, P(An)}
max{P(A1), P(A2), …, P(An)} ≤ P(A1 ∨ A2 ∨ … ∨ An) ≤ min{1, P(A1) + P(A2) + … + P(An)}

If A and B are independent, then we can plot exact values for P(A, B) or P(A ∨ B):
Truth FunctionExact Value
ConjunctionP(A) · P(B)
DisjunctionP(A) + P(B) - P(A) · P(B)

There are some interesting observations on the visualizations. Bounds get vanishingly tight at the edges of the unit square. The range plots make this very clear and they are the exact same plots for both P(A, B) and P(A ∨ B). Notice that the center holds the widest bounds. Every plot is symmetric around the P(B) = P(A) diagonal, this should be expected as P(A) and P(B) play interchangeable rolls in Fréchet inequalities.

Maurice Fréchet

Maurice Fréchet was a significant French mathematician with contributions to topology of point sets, metric spaces, statistics and probability, and calculus7. Fréchet published the above inequalities in the French journal Fundamenta Mathaticae in 19358. During that time, he was Professor and Chair of Differential and Integral Calculus at the Sorbonne9.

Jacques Fréchet, Maurice's father, was head of a school in Paris10 while Maurice was young. Maurice then went to secondary school where he was taught math by the legendary French mathematician Jacques Hadamard. Hadamard would soon after become a professor at the University of Bordeaux. Eventually, Hadamard would become Fréchet's advisor for his doctorate. An educator like his father, Maurice was a schoolteacher in 1907, a lecturer in 1908, and then a professor in 191011. Probability research came later in his life. Unfortunately, his work wasn't always appreciated as the renowned Swedish mathematician Harald Cramér wrote12:

In early years Fréchet had been an outstanding mathematician, doing pathbreaking work in functional analysis. He had taken up probabilistic work at a fairly advanced age, and I am bound to say that his work in this field did not seem very impressive to me.

Nevertheless, Fréchet would go on to become very influential in probability and statistics. As a great response to Cramér's former criticism, an important bound is named after both Fréchet and Cramér, the Fréchet–Darmois–Cramér–Rao inequality (though more commonly known as Cramér–Rao bound)!
